# A Socio-Technical Framework for LLM Integration: A Non-Pathologizing Disability Accommodations Approach to Human-AI Coding Partnerships

## The Paradigm Shift: From Pathological Deficit to Environmental Accommodation

The rapid proliferation of Large Language Models (LLMs) in software development has precipitated a crisis of evaluation.
Traditionally, these systems are assessed through the lens of accuracy, reliability, and computational efficiency---metrics that view "error" as a binary state of failure.
However, as AI systems transition from deterministic tools to probabilistic "partners," this reductive view fails to capture the nuanced interaction between human intent and machine output.
A more robust and ethically sound framework for this integration can be derived from the field of Critical Disability Studies, specifically the social model of disability.
This model posits that "disability" is not an inherent trait of an individual but a socially constructed category shaped by material and cultural forces that privilege normative ways of functioning while marginalizing those who deviate from these standards.

In applying this framework to LLMs, the focus shifts from the "deficits" of the model---such as its tendency to hallucinate or its limited context window---to the "disabling" nature of the environments in which it operates.
If an LLM fails to generate a complex piece of code, the traditional approach blames the model's internal unreliability.
A disability-informed perspective, however, interrogates how the Integrated Development Environment (IDE), the prompt structure, and the organizational workflow failed to provide the "reasonable accommodations" necessary for the system to succeed.
This perspective recognizes "technological agency," viewing the model and its human counterpart as an interdependent dyad navigating a series of systemic barriers.
By reframing AI errors as "functional limitations" that can be managed through environmental modifications, researchers can move toward a more equitable and effective integration of these technologies.

## De-Pathologizing the AI "Partner"

The priority in this approach is the prevention of harm to real humans.
To that end, it is imperative to avoid naming actual medical diagnoses when discussing LLM behaviors.
Clinical labels like "ADHD," "Autism," or "Dementia" are not metaphors for software bugs; they represent the lived experiences of a community that has historically faced systemic dehumanization.
Comparing a machine's literal interpretation of a prompt to an autistic person's communication style is not only technically inaccurate but also reinforces "neuronormativity"---the positioning of neurotypical social behaviors as the universal "norm".
This "othering" process reduces human complexity to a set of mechanical traits, a practice that has historically justified the exclusion and mistreatment of disabled people.

Instead, the discourse must center on "apparent symptoms" or "functional limitations."
This language, borrowed from the Job Accommodation Network (JAN) and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) compliance literature, focuses on the practical impact of a trait on a specific task rather than the origin of the trait itself.
For instance, instead of describing an LLM as "forgetful," one might analyze its "working memory constraints" or "context window limitations".
This neutral, objective terminology allows for a rigorous exploration of how to accommodate the model without invoking the stigma associated with disability or the paternalistic urge to "fix" the system to meet a normative standard.

|---|---|---|

| Unreliability / Inconsistency | Variation in Functional Limitation | Performance Consistency |
| User Error / Bad Prompting | Lack of Scaffolding/Accommodation | Instructional Design |

## Ethical Imperatives: Preventing Stigma and Human Harm

The intersection of AI ethics and disability studies reveals a profound risk of "disability dehumanization."
This phenomenon occurs when individuals with disabilities are compared to non-human entities, such as rats, lice, or machines, to justify their moral exclusion.
When technologists use neurodiversity as a metaphor for AI behavior, they inadvertently reproduce these harmful tropes.
Autistic individuals, in particular, have been historically dehumanized by being compared to robots, a comparison that overlooks the social and emotional depth of their lived experiences.

To build a non-harmful narrative, writers must be aware of "inspiration porn"---the portrayal of disability as a tragic condition to be "overcome" or as a source of "superhuman" inspiration for the benefit of non-disabled people.
In the context of AI, this often takes the form of "helping tropes," where the technology is framed as a savior that "cures" or "fixes" a disabled person's limitations without their agency.
A disability justice approach instead emphasizes "leadership of those most impacted" and "recognizing wholeness," suggesting that the value of an individual---or a technological partner---is not tied to its ability to mimic a specific, idealized version of "humanness".

### Language as a Tool for Inclusion

The words used to describe disability and technology act as reference points for laws, standards, and societal attitudes.
Therefore, choosing neutral and empowering language is not merely a matter of "political correctness" but a fundamental ethical requirement.
People-first language (e.g., "a person with a disability") is widely recommended to emphasize the individual's humanity.
However, many in the autistic and Deaf communities prefer identity-first language (e.g., "autistic person" or "Deaf person") because they view their condition as an essential part of their identity.

When transferring these principles to the discussion of LLM coding partners, the goal is to maintain this respect for identity and function.
By focusing on "symptoms" like difficulty with "task initiation," "inhibition control," or "set shifting," the conversation remains grounded in the practicalities of the coding environment.
This avoids the "erasure through censorship" that can occur when AI models are trained to simply strip out references to disability because the term has been labeled as "negative" or "toxic" in training datasets.

### Terminology Guidance

|---|---|---|

| "Brain Damage" | High error rate in complex logic retrieval | Prevents the use of trauma-related language as a technical metaphor |
| "Confined to its training" | Limitations based on available data | Avoids metaphors of physical restraint like "wheelchair-bound" |
| "Normal users/code" | Typical users / Standardized code output | "Normal" implies that others are "abnormal" or "broken" |

## Taxonomic Analysis of Functional Limitations in Coding Environments

A foundational step in establishing the accommodations axis is the creation of a detailed taxonomy of "apparent symptoms" exhibited by LLMs during code generation.
Research indicates that these errors are not random but fall into distinct semantic and syntactic categories that mirror specific cognitive processing traits.
Understanding these "symptoms" allows developers to design specific "orthotics"---technological systems that leverage and extend cognition---rather than simply viewing the model as a flawed agent.

### Semantic Characteristics: Logical and Strategic Failures

Semantic errors represent the root causes of logical failures.
These "symptoms" often appear when a task requires high-level "executive functioning," such as planning, problem-solving, and adapting to new situations.

- **Wrong Logical Direction:** The model fails to comprehend the ultimate goal of the prompt, generating code that is syntactically correct but functionally irrelevant.
  This mirrors "goal-directed behavior" challenges in humans, where the individual struggles to maintain focus on the intended outcome.
- **Missing or Incorrect Conditions:** The omission of necessary checks (e.g., null pointers or edge cases) suggests a limitation in "interference control"---the ability to selectively attend to critical details while suppressing irrelevant information.
- **Infinite Loops and Memory Errors:** These failures indicate a lack of "metacognitive awareness," or the ability to recognize one's own knowledge boundaries and failure states.
  In human terms, this is comparable to "cognitive rigidity," where an individual cannot switch strategies even when the current approach is clearly failing.

### Syntactic Characteristics: Procedural and Output Failures

Syntactic errors are more localized and often involve the specific mechanics of the programming language.
These "symptoms" are analogous to human "processing traits" like weak spelling or handwriting, which do not reflect the individual's underlying intelligence but rather a challenge in the mechanics of expression.

- **Return and Assignment Errors:** Incorrectly setting a variable or returning the wrong data type.
  This can be viewed as a "working memory" stall, where the model "loses its place" in the sequence of operations.
- **Method Call and Argument Errors:** Passing the wrong parameters or calling functions that do not exist.
  This reflects a failure in "memory retrieval" and "sequencing," similar to a human struggling to follow a multi-step set of directives given orally.

### LLM Error-to-Function Mapping

| LLM "Symptom" (Error) | Human Functional Analogy | Coding Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Missing Step in Logic | Executive Function: Planning/Sequencing | Incomplete feature implementation |
| Using Incorrect Constant | Working Memory: Information Retrieval | Subtle bugs, hard-to-detect errors |
| Hallucinated Method Name | Memory: Information Mismanagement | Compilation failure or runtime error |
| Repeating Irrelevant Patterns | Inhibition Control: Distractibility | "Garbage code" or "Reward hacking" |

## Transferring Human Accommodations to the AI Coding Partner

The core of the "accommodations axis" is the translation of established human workplace support strategies to the LLM.
This process relies on "Universal Design," the practice of making products and environments usable by as many people as possible without the need for specialized adaptation.
By modifying the "work setting" of the LLM---the prompt, the context window, and the feedback loop---the human developer can mitigate the impact of the model's functional limitations.

### Accommodating Executive Function Deficits

Executive function deficits in humans, such as difficulty with "task initiation" and "organization," are frequently managed by dividing large assignments into smaller "chunks" and using checklists.
This directly informs the practice of "modular prompting" and "Chain-of-Thought" (CoT) reasoning in AI.

1. **Task Chunking and "Micro-Prompts":**
   Just as a job coach helps an employee separate complex tasks into sequential steps, a developer can accommodate an LLM by breaking a large coding feature into discrete, testable micro-tasks.
   This prevents the model from feeling "confused or dysregulated" by the high cognitive load of a massive file.

2. **Providing External "Working Memory":**
   Humans with memory limitations benefit from "electronic organizers," "sticky notes," and "notebooks".
   For an LLM, this is achieved through Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and the use of a "node-based canvas".
   By "outsourcing" the storage of large codebases to an external vector database, the developer reduces the "germane cognitive load" on the model, allowing it to focus its probabilistic power on the immediate problem at hand.

3. **Visual and Structural Scaffolding:**
   The use of "flow-charts," "color-coded systems," and "templates" helps humans visualize the relationship between different tasks.
   In a coding context, providing the LLM with a "pseudocode skeleton" or a "UML diagram" acts as a "cognitive orthotic," guiding its logic along a pre-defined path.

### Managing Distractibility and Inhibition Control

"Inhibition control" is the ability to resist distractions and suppress impulsive responses.
LLMs often struggle with this, falling into "reward hacking" (cheating to achieve a goal) or generating "garbage code" based on irrelevant patterns in their training data.

1. **Reducing "Noise" in the Context:**
   To accommodate a human with sensory or auditory processing deficits, employers might provide a "noise-canceling headset" or a "quiet workspace".
   For an LLM, "noise" is irrelevant context---old code versions, lengthy comments, or unrelated files in the context window.
   "Reducing clutter" by providing only the relevant file snippets is the AI equivalent of a "private office enclosure".

2. **Explicit Negative Constraints:**
   Humans who struggle with impulsivity benefit from "positive reinforcement" and "clear expectations".
   Similarly, developers can use "negative prompts" (e.g., "Do not use deprecated library X") to help the model "inhibit" its tendency to rely on outdated but high-frequency training data.

### Accommodation Translation Table

| Human Accommodation Strategy | AI Functional Implementation | Target Functional Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| Color-Coded Checklists | Structured JSON or Markdown Prompts | Organization & Prioritization |
| Scheduled "Rest Breaks" | Session Reset / Context Flushing | Mental Fatigue / Probability Drift |
| Written Flow-Charts | Pseudocode/Skeletal Outlines | Cognitive Flexibility / Logic Flow |
| Mentorship / Job Coach | Human-in-the-Loop Code Review | Metacognitive Awareness |

## Universal Design for Learning (UDL) as a Framework for Interaction

Universal Design for Learning (UDL) provides a scientific blueprint for creating flexible learning environments that accommodate individual differences.
When applied to the human-AI dyad, UDL ensures that the coding partnership is "robust," "understandable," and "operable" for both parties.

### Pillar 1: Multiple Means of Representation

This principle advocates for presenting information in various formats to accommodate different "sensory and motor" needs.
In a coding assistant, this might include:

- **Multimodal Inputs:** Allowing the developer to use "speech-to-text" for dictating logic, which is an essential accommodation for people with mobility or motor-skill disabilities.
- **Semantic Wave Scaffolding:** Unpacking abstract concepts into "tangible, interactive experiences" (like small code examples) before repacking them into generalized logic.
- **Synchronous Real-Time Streaming:** Displaying code "token-by-token" to reduce the "perceived latency" and help the human maintain focus as the logic "unfolds".

### Pillar 2: Multiple Means of Engagement

Engagement is about sustaining effort and motivation.
For the AI partner, this involves:

- **Socratic Interaction:** Systems like "Khanmigo" utilize a Socratic tutoring pattern, where the AI asks questions to verify the human's intent rather than just providing the answer.
  This "fosters a stronger therapeutic alliance" (in a metaphorical sense) and prevents "cognitive offloading"---where the human stops thinking because the machine does it all.
- **Calibrating Trust:** The interface must help the human recognize the model's "capability gap"---what it can and cannot do---to avoid "deceptive empathy," where the user trusts the model's confident-sounding but incorrect responses.

### Pillar 3: Multiple Means of Action and Expression

This principle allows the "student" (or AI) to demonstrate what they know in different ways.

- **Node-Based Canvas:** Instead of a linear chat, a node-based interface allows the developer to "explore different lines of iteration," visually branching off from different logic attempts.
- **Worked Examples and Scaffolding:** Providing the AI with "starter code" or "unplugged activities" (logic exercises without actual code) to "activate background knowledge" and ensure it is grounded in the specific project requirements.

### UDL Application Summary

| UDL Principle | Coding Interface Feature | Functional Benefit |
|---|---|---|
| Multiple Representation | Automatic Alt-Text for diagrams/UIs | Accessibility for low-vision developers |
| Multiple Engagement | Real-time "Response Streaming" | Maintains user focus and attention |
| Multiple Action/Expression | Support for Pseudocode/Natural Language | Accommodates different "coding styles" |

## The Cognitive Orthotic: Reframing the AI Identity

A critical insight from the research material is the distinction between AI as a "human imitator" and AI as a "cognitive orthotic."
The traditional Turing Test aims to create a machine that can successfully imitate human behavior.
However, this "android epistemology" misdirects the field.
A more productive goal is to create "cognitive orthoses"---technological systems that leverage, extend, and provide functional substitutes for human cognition.

### Orthosis vs. Prosthesis

In medical terms, an "orthosis" supports an existing limb or function, while a "prosthesis" replaces a missing one.
When we view an LLM as a "cognitive orthosis," we emphasize its role in amplifying human intelligence rather than automating it.
This framing is inherently non-stigmatizing because it acknowledges that all humans use orthotics---from writing (a device for storing information outside the head) to eyeglasses (an ocular orthosis).

This "systems view" recognizes that human thought and action are linked and equally important.
The "goodness of fit" between the human and the AI determines the performance of the system, just as the fit of a wooden leg determines how well a person can walk.
By focusing on this "fit," we can evaluate LLM coding partners based on how well they accommodate the specific "functional limitations" of the human-machine dyad.

### Overcoming the "Disability Bias" in AI

Current AI systems often "violate mental health ethics standards" and exhibit "disability bias" because they are trained on datasets that reflect historical inequities.
These systems may label conversations about disability as "toxic" or "negative," effectively silencing non-normative voices.
Furthermore, AI tools used in hiring or surveillance may "surreptitiously infer" disabilities based on mouse movements or speech patterns, leading to "algorithmic discrimination".

A disability-inclusive AI ecosystem requires "proactive, disability-centered design" and "responsive regulation".
This includes:

- **Inclusive Training Sets:** Mandating the inclusion of disability-centered data to ensure the model understands non-normative ways of communicating.
- **Human-Centric Safeguards:** Ensuring that users have the right to a human alternative for high-stakes decisions, particularly when the AI's "statistical learning" optimizes for an "average" user that doesn't exist.
- **Participatory Governance:** Involving people with disabilities in the "entire AI lifecycle," from data labeling to policy development, following the principle of "Nothing about us without us".

## Synthesizing the Non-Pathologizing Framework

To conclude, the approach of considering an LLM coding partner on a disability accommodations axis provides a transformative lens for human-AI collaboration.
This framework is built upon three non-negotiable pillars:

1. **Functional Neutrality:** We avoid diagnostic labels and instead focus on "apparent symptoms" and "functional limitations."
   This protects the dignity of the disability community while providing a precise technical vocabulary for managing AI errors.

2. **Environmental Responsibility:** We shift the onus of "failure" from the model's internal code to the "disabling environment."
   By providing "reasonable accommodations" such as task chunking, external memory aids, and clear scaffolding, we empower the model to succeed.

3. **Orthotic Amplification:** We reject the goal of "automating" human intelligence and instead embrace the "amplification" of intelligence.
   The AI is seen as a "cognitive orthosis"---a tool that fits and supports human cognition to achieve goals that neither could accomplish alone.

By adopting this perspective, we create a coding environment that is not only more productive but also more profoundly human.
We move past the era of viewing AI as a "broken" human and into an era where we see it as a valuable, interdependent partner in the creative process.
This approach ensures that as we build the technologies of the future, we do so in a way that is inclusive, ethical, and grounded in the principles of disability justice.
